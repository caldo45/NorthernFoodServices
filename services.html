Food Safety & H.A.C.C.P

Food safety is of paramount important to all food businesses to ensure the preparation, packaging, distribution and sale of safe food and the prevention of food borne illness amongst consumers.

HACCP is a systematic approach to controlling the risks from food safety hazards, employed by food businesses throughout the world and mandatory under EU and UK food law. It involves the application of the 7 Codex Alimentarius Principles to systematically analyse processes within food businesses to identify all food safety hazards (physical, chemical, microbiological). These hazards are subsequently evaluated to determine which are significant for food safety and control applied at specific steps in the process to control them to ensure food safety.

“Food business operators shall put in place, implement and maintain a permanent procedure or procedures based on the HACCP principles”.
REGULATION (EC) No 852/2004 on the hygiene of foodstuffs (Chapter II Article 5 Point 1).

  • Provide complete HACCP System development and implementation from Codex Alimentarius Principle 1 through to Principle 7 including the various application steps.

    • Principle 1 – conduct a hazard analysis listing all the potential hazards at each process step and consider measures to control each.

Food Legislation &
Regulatory Compliance

There is a considerable body of legislation, codes of practice and guidance documents that regulate foodstuffs. These cover all parts of the food production and distribution chain, including:

  • Production
  • Processing
  • Packaging and Labelling
  • Importing
  • Distribution
  • Retailing and Catering

EU regulations are provided with enforcement powers through national legislation in each of the devolved regions of the UK and in Ireland.

By its nature legislation can be complex, lengthy, ambiguous and with the introduction of new and amendments to existing regulations this can easily lead to non-compliance.

Offences committed, whether deliberate or accidental, may result in prosecution with the consequential financial losses, reputational damage and potential product recall which can ultimately result in the closure of the business.

Food Security, Authenticity & Supply Chain Integrity

Due to a number of high-profile incidents involving food adulteration and substitution which have occurred in recent years, international attention has been drawn to issues such as Food Security, Authenticity & Supply Chain Integrity.

Such incidents have caused significant reputational damage to the food industry not to mention consumer confidence in our food, calling into question its authenticity and origin.

These incidents have also highlighted susceptibilities due to the complexity in our modern-day food supply chain networks and have forced the industry to devise and apply protocols to establish control in this area.

Food traceability is already enshrined in law and having in place a robust and fully transparent traceability system is a key element which can be utilised to identify and mitigate any risks which may have the potential to compromise the integrity of any raw ingredient at any point in the supply chain network.

Food and feed business operators shall be able to identify any person from whom they have been supplied with a food, a feed, a food-producing animal, or any substance intended to be, or expected to be, incorporated into a food or feed.
REGULATION (EC) No 178/2002 laying down the general principles and requirements of food law (Chapter II, Section 3, Article 18)

Modern food businesses now fully understand the need to prioritise food security, have full confidence in the integrity of their supply chain and be assured of the origin of all raw ingredients utilised within their production processes.

Developed in response to the various incidents which have taken place are a number of protocols available to food businesses to enable them to enhance food security by identifying vulnerabilities and threats in their supply chain and mitigate against risks such as food adulteration/contamination by either deliberate (food fraud) or accidental means.

NFS Can:

  • Independently and systematically assess the integrity of your existing supply chain network to identify potential threats and vunerabilities.

  • Assess whether a coherent and robust traceability system is in place.

  • Conduct supply chain mapping for individual ingredients.

  • Introduce various risk-based protocols e.g. V.A.C.C.P. (Vunerability Assessment Critical Control Point) or T.A.C.C.P. (Threat Assessment Critical Control Point) and predictive modelling tools such as Horizon Scanning into your business.

  • Review and enhance your current Supplier Approval Protocols and inspection & testing regimes to further boost food defence.

Environmental &
Operational Hygiene

Satisfactory standards of hygiene during the preparation, packaging, storage, distribution and offering for sale of foodstuffs is a legal requirement.

The presence of harmful micro-organisms on food-contact surfaces and/or present in the food processing environment is a serious concern for all food businesses.

Pathogenic micro-organisms such as Listeria spp. E Coli, Campylobacter spp. or Salmonella spp. may be present due inadequate or ineffective hygiene practices and if transferred to the food have the potential to cause serious illness or death amongst consumers

“Food premises are to be kept clean and maintained in good repair and condition"
REGULATION (EC) No 852/2004 on the hygiene of foodstuffs Annex II, Chapter I, Point 1)
“All articles, fittings and equipment with which food comes into contact are to: (a) be effectively cleaned and, where necessary, disinfected. Cleaning and disinfection are to take place at a frequency sufficient to avoid any risk of contamination;”
REGULATION (EC) No 852/2004 on the hygiene of foodstuffs Annex II, Chapter V, Point I)
